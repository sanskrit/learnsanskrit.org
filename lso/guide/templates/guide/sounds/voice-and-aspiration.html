The stop letters are simple sounds produced in a simple way:

    {{ 'ka'|sound('"k" in "skill"') }}

But as with many things in life, these letters become more interesting when they become more complex. For one, we can make a stop voiced:

    {{ 'ga'|sound('"g" in "gill"') }}

or unvoiced, like {{ 'ka'|i }}. These letters are in English, too. You can feel the difference between these two letters by touching your windpipe while you produce them.

But Sanskrit complicates the stops letters in a second way. Recall that a stop is produced when the flow of air stops then quickly resumes. In {{ 'ka'|i }}, this flow resumes normally, like water from a tap. But this flow can also resume explosively, like water bursting through a dam:

    {{ 'kha'|sound('"k" in "kill"') }}

Sounds like {{ 'kha'|i }} are aspirated ("breathy"), and sounds like {{ 'ka'|i }} are unaspirated ("not breathy"). And of course, these aspirated letters can be voiced, too:

    {{ 'gha'|sound('(no match)') }}

Blending stops

Stops blend very easily, whether with nasals:

    {{ ex('tat na Asyam', 'That is not a mouth.', iast=true) }} {{ ex('rAT naraH', 'The king is a man.', iast=true) }}

or with most other letters. Stops become voiced in front of any voiced letter, including vowels:

    {{ ex('tat asat -> tad asat', 'That is false.', iast=true) }} {{ ex('vAk eva -> vAg eva', 'speech itself', iast=true) }}

semivowels:

    {{ ex('tat yacchati -> tad yacchati', 'He restrains it.', iast=true) }}

and other stops:

    {{ ex('sa rAT bhavati -> sa rAD bhavati', 'He becomes a king.', iast=true) }}

but like a diamond in the mud, these stops keep their value: they use the same point of sound.

As before, stops only blend between words, not inside them. Otherwise, we would become hopelessly confused:

    {{ ex('tan mantram', 'That is a mantra.', iast=true) }} {{ ex('tan mandram', 'That is charming.', iast=true) }}
