Now we bring our study of the eight cases to a close. Together, the eight cases express a variety of relationships and can fill in the details of some action.

Case 7

Case 7 defines the location of the action.

    {{ iex('loke caranti mRgAH', 'The deer roam in the world.') }} {{ iex('bhavanti siMhA grAmayoH', 'Lions are in the two villages.') }} {{ iex('gRheSu jAyante putrAH', 'Sons are born in the houses.') }}

As usual, we can use multiple cases at once:

    {{ ihex('gRhe devaM pazyAmi', 'I see the god in the house.') }} {{ ihex('loke narA bhavanti', 'The men are in the world.') }} {{ iex('grAme devAH putrAMz corayanti', 'The gods steal the sons in the village.') }}

As an aside, note that {{ 'bhavati'|i }} can use two nouns in case 1:

    {{ iex('narasya putrA devA bhavanti', "The man's sons are gods.") }}

{{ '-e'|i }} sandhi

The letter {{ 'e'|i }} behaves simple enough:

    {{ iex('gRhe pacadhve', 'You all cook for yourselves in the house', hint=true) }} {{ iex('gRhe jAyete putrau', "The two sons are born in the house.", hint=true) }} {{ iex('gRhe manyAmahe', 'We think in the house.', hint=true) }}

Still, what happens when {{ 'e'|i }} is near vowels? This, too, is familiar to us already: a sentence like {{ 'grAme icchanti'|i }} will become {{ 'grAmayicchanti'|i }}. But when this blending occurred between two words, Sanskrit speakers went one step further. They thought {{ 'y'|i }} was a tiresome letter to have to say between these two words, so they removed it entirely:

    {{ iex('grAme icchanti -> grAma icchanti', 'They want in the village.', hint=true) }}

But consider a phrase like {{ 'kRSNaH azvaH'|i }}, which becomes {{ "kRSNo 'zvaH"|i }}. {{ 'aH'|i }} overwhelms the {{ 'a'|i }}, and {{ 'e'|i }} does the same.

    {{ iex("gRhe azvaH bhavati -> gRhe 'zvo bhavati", 'The horse is in the house.', hint=true) }}

This change is unusual and against our Sanskrit intuition. But it is the sort of change that we must just accept.

Case 8

Apart from case 6, the cases we have seen so far all define different parts of a given action. But case 8 steps beyond this action and defines the person who hears the sentence. We could also say that it defines the person we refer to with the word "you."

    {{ iex('putra vIro bhavasi', 'Son, you are a hero.') }} {{ iex('narau siMhAn pazyAmi', 'O you (two) men, I see the lions.') }} {{ iex('icchAmy azvaM devAH ', "O gods, I want a horse.") }}

The forms of case 8 are largely identical to the forms of case 1. But instead of {{ 'naraH'|i }}, we have {{ 'nara'|i }}. This means that {{ 'nara'|i }} can blend with the words that follow it:

    {{ ihex('narAzvaM corayati', 'O man, he is stealing a horse.') }}

The eight cases

We have now studied all eight of the Sanskrit noun cases. Apart from case 6 and case 8, these cases define the roles that certain nouns play in the verb action:

    {{ iex('vIrA gacchanti gRhAd azvair grAmAn narAya', 'The heroes go with the horses from the house to the villages for the man.') }}

Case 6 connects two nouns:

    {{ iex('narasya gajam pazyAmaH', "We see the man's elephant.") }}

And case 8 addresses the listener:

    {{ iex('nara pazyAmi sundaram', "O man, I see a beautiful one.") }}

Usually, these forms are assembled in a table, like so:

{{ nominal('gaja', 'm') }}

You know all of these forms already. But for the sake of practice, learn to recite these forms in order, from {{ 'gajaH'|i }} and {{ 'gajau'|i }} in case 1 all the way to {{ 'gajAH'|i }} in case 8. Doing so will fix these forms in your mind.

Ambiguity

Tables are not a fun way to study Sanskrit, but they can make certain trends very clear. Here, for example, we see all of the forms that are used multiple times:

    {{ iex('gajau', '(a) The two elephants (case 1)
    (b) The two elephants (case 2)
    (c) The two elephants (case 8)',) }} {{ iex('gajAH', '(a) The elephants (case 1)
    (b) The elephants (case 8)') }} {{ iex('gajAbhyAm', '(a) With the two elephants (case 3)
    (b) For the two elephants (case 4)
    (c) From the two elephants (case 5)') }} {{ iex('gajebhyaH', '(a) For the elephants (case 4)
    (b) From the elephants (case 5)') }} {{ iex('gajayoH', '(a) Of the two elephants (case 6)
    (b) In the two elephants (case 7)') }}

As always, we should rely on context and common sense to help us:

    {{ ihex('nara icchati', 'The man wants.') }}

Distribution

Sanskrit has a reputation for being an impossibly hard language. Many people think of it as a language that ordinary people can't learn. But that reputation is misleading: as long as you know how to study it, Sanskrit is not a tough language. The key is to focus on the most useful and common parts of the language first. So when you study some Sanskrit concept, you should always ask: how often is this really used?

For example, how often are the different noun cases used? Which are the most important ones to know? This answer can vary from text to text. But in the Bhagavad Gita, the results are quite clear:
