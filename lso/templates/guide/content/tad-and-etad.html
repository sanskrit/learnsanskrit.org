Review ------ A noun's ending tells us three things: the *case* of the noun, the *number* of things referred to, and the *gender* of the noun. We have seen nouns in all eight cases, all three numbers, and all three genders: !!! examples - {{ m.x('naraH', 'a man (case 1)') }} - {{ m.x('phalam', 'a fruit (case 1)') }} - {{ m.x('senA', 'an army (case 1)') }} A vague stem ------------ The noun and adjective stems that we've seen are quite specific. A `nara` is a man. Something that's `kRSNa` is black. But there are other words that hardly have this meaning at all: !!! examples - {{ m.x('saH', 'He.') }} - {{ m.x('tena', 'With them.') }} - {{ m.x('tAm', 'Her.') }} Such words are called pronouns in English grammar, and the term is useful here too. Unlike a noun or adjective stem, a pronoun stem is almost meaningless. I say *almost* meaningless because pronoun stems have a sort of vague and general meaning in Sanskrit. More precisely, the examples above mean the following: !!! examples - {{ m.x('saH', 'That one. (masculine, case 1)') }} - {{ m.x('taiH', 'With those ones.') }} - {{ m.x('tAm', 'That one. (feminine, case 2)') }} But this meaning is subtle and often ignored. `tad` ----- The most common pronoun by far is `tad`. In general, we use it as if its stem is `ta` and its feminine stem is `tA`. {% filter markdown %} ### Blending `saH` In front of consonants, `saH` becomes `sa`: !!! examples - {{ m.x('sa gacchati', 'He goes.') }} Otherwise, it's used normally: !!! examples - {{ m.x('so \'zvaH', 'That is a horse.') }} - {{ m.x('sa icchati', 'He wants.') }} {% endfilter %}
`etad` ------ `etad` is much rarer than `tad`. Whereas `tad` implies that something is far away, `etad` implies that something is very close by. To get the forms for `etad`, we simply stick `e` on the front of all the `tad` forms: !!! examples - {{ m.x('tena -> etena', 'With him (far). -> With him (near).') }} - {{ m.x('sA -> eSA', 'She (far). -> She (near).') }}
