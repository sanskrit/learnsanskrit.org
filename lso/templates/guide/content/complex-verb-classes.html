Review ------ Verbs are formed from verb stems, which are formed from verb roots. Roots are sorted into ten different *classes*. A root's class tells us how to turn a root into a verb in the present tense. We've seen four classes so far. These are the *strong `a` class*: !!! examples - {{ m.x('bhU -> bhavati', 'He is.') }} The *weak `a` class*: !!! examples - {{ m.x('tud -> tudati', 'He strikes.') }} The *`ya` class*: !!! examples - {{ m.x('muh -> muhyati', 'He is deluded.') }} And the *`aya` class*: !!! examples - {{ m.x('cur -> corayati', 'He steals.') }} Simple and complex classes -------------------------- The four verb classes that we've seen so far are simple in various ways. But the six remaining verb classes are complex in various ways: ### Blending The complex verb classes use stems that don't end in `a`. These vowels often cause complex sound changes: !!! examples - {{ m.x('tiSTha + si -> tiSThasi', 'You stand.') }} - {{ m.x('karo + si -> karoSi', 'You do.') }} - {{ m.x('tano + si -> tanoSi', 'You spread out.') }} Here, the rule is a simple one: !!! rule If `s` appears after a vowel that's not `a` or `A`, it becomes `S`. This is the same rule that gives us `gajeSu` instead of `*gajesu`. But there are others that are trickier: !!! examples - {{ m.x('yuj -> yunakti', 'yoke -> He yokes.') }} We'll look at some of these changes later on. ### Multiple stems The complex verb classes often **use multiple stems**: !!! examples - {{ m.x('zRNoti', 'He hears.') }} - {{ m.x('zRNutaH', 'The two of them hear.') }} They have a strong stem that's used for certain forms in `parasmaipada` and a weak stem that's used everywhere else. Unfortunately, I don't know why they use multiple stems at all.
