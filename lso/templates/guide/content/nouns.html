Generally, every Sanskrit sentence describes an *action*. The simplest sentences are just actions and nothing else: !!! examples - {{ m.x('pazyati', 'He sees.')}} Unfortunately, these sentences tell us very little. If we see just `pazyati`, how can we tell who sees, or what is seen? How and where does this "seeing" happen? To fill these gaps, we use nouns: !!! examples - {{ m.x('[gajaH] pazyati', '[The elephant] sees.')}} Nouns describe ideas as simple as elephants and fruits, and as complex as places and concepts. Stems and endings ----------------- Just like verbs, nouns have a stem, which combines with an ending to make a complete word: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gajaH pazyati', 'The elephant sees.') }} - {{ m.x('gajau pazyataH', 'The two elephants see.') }} - {{ m.x('gajAH pazyanti', 'The elephants see.') }} - {{ m.x('naraH pacate', 'The man cooks for himself.') }} - {{ m.x('narau pacete', 'The two men cook for themselves.') }} - {{ m.x('narAH pacante', 'The men cook for themselves.') }} - {{ m.x('azvas tiSThati (azvaH tiSThati)', 'The horse stands.') }} - {{ m.x('azvau tiSThataH', 'The two horses stand.') }} - {{ m.x('azvAs tiSThanti (azvAH tiSThanti)', 'The horses stand.') }} !!! note Note the blending that occurs in `azvas tiSThati` and `azvAs tiSThati`. Now that our sentences have multiple words, we have to be mindful of how the sounds in the two words affect each other. Noun roles ---------- In each example above, the noun defines the thing that "does" the action. But nouns can define other parts of the action, too. With `pazyati`, for example, we can define what is seen: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gajaH pazyati [naram]', 'The elephant sees [the man].') }} Or where the elephant sees: !!! examples - {{ m.x('[grAme] gajaH pazyati', 'The elephant sees [in the village].') }} Or what the elephant sees with: !!! examples - {{ m.x('[azvena] gajaH pazyati', 'The elephant sees [with the horse].') }} Here, the role of the noun changes when we change the noun ending. So in addition to showing the *number* of items involved, the noun ending shows the noun's role. Generally, we can call these roles cases. But in later lessons, we'll see that roles and cases are not exactly the same.
