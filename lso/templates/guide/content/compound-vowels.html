Non-similar vowels ------------------ As we've already seen, simple vowels can combine in interesting ways: !!! examples - {{ m.x('sA Apnoti -> sApnoti') }} - {{ m.x('gacchati IzvaraH -> gacchatIzvaraH') }} In these examples, the vowels are in the same "group": `a` combines with `A`, and `i` combines with `I`. That is, the vowels that combine are similar. But what if the two vowels *aren't* similar? !!! examples - {{ m.x('sakhA iti matvA') }} - {{ m.x('gacchati azvaH') }} For now, let's focus on the first example. Let's also look at another similar example: !!! examples - {{ m.x('mama icchA') }} - {{ m.x('sakhA iti matvA') }} In other words, what happens when `a` or `A` is the first vowel? `a/A` + simple vowel -------------------- Sanskrit vowels tend to blend with each other. And the same thing happens here. But instead of getting another simple vowel, we get a new kind of vowel: !!! examples - {{ m.x('mama icchA -> mamecchA') }} - {{ m.x('mama udakam -> mamodakam') }} Let's call these new vowels compound vowels, since they're combinations of different kinds of vowels. When you pronounce `e` or `o`, try to make the sound "flat" and constant. If you are a native English speaker, this might be hard; it is tempting to pronounce `e` as if it were `ei` and `o` as if it is were `ou`. `e` and `o` are both long vowels, like `A`, `I`, and the others. How do the vowels blend? `a/A` + `e/o` ------------- When `a` and `i` combine, the two vowels blend and combine into a single `e`. But if we add another `a`, then the `a` sound becomes stronger and forces the sounds to separate: !!! examples - {{ m.x('a + e -> ai') }} This idea applies more generally to any combination of `a/A` and `e/o`: !!! examples - {{ m.x('a + e -> ai') }} - {{ m.x('A + e -> ai') }} - {{ m.x('a + o -> au') }} - {{ m.x('A + o -> au') }} And because of this process, we get two more compound vowels: {{ m.sounds( ('ai', 'ai', '"i" in "fight"'), ('au', 'au', '"ow" in "cow"'), ) }} (`R`, `RR`, and `lR` can't make compound vowels. We`ll see why in a later lesson.) !!! note As you pronounce `ai` and `au`, try to make the "a" part of `ai` and `au` sound just like the vowel `a`. The shorter it is, the better. Now, the natural next question is whether we can combine `ai` and `au` once more. It is easy to imagine what this might look like: !!! examples - {{ m.x('a/A + ai -> Ai ?') }} - {{ m.x('a/A + au -> Au ?') }} But alas, Sanskrit does not work this way. `ai` and `au` "combine," but they remain unchanged: !!! examples - {{ m.x('a/A + ai -> ai') }} - {{ m.x('a/A + au -> au') }} This is probably for the best; without a firm limit, perhaps Sanskrit would end up with vowels like `AAAAAAi`, or something else equally painful.
