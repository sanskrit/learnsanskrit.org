Review ------ We create *compounds* by removing a noun's ending and combining it with another noun. !!! examples - {{ m.x('[grAmavIraM] rakSAmi', 'I protect the [village hero].') }} We lose *freedom* and *clarity* by doing this, but we get a *density* of expression that is hard to achieve otherwise. "One who has" ------------- There's a new type of compound that's easily confused with the `tatpuruSa`. It can be hard to understand. But fortunately, it exists in English too. Read over the words below: - flatfoot - lowlife - blockhead There's a subtle pattern at work here. A flatfoot isn't a *foot*; it's someone who *has* flat feet. A lowlife isn't a *life*; it's someone who *has* a low way of life. And a blockhead isn't a *head*; it's someone who *has* a head that's like a block. Our new compound follows the same pattern: !!! examples - {{ m.x('kRSNagajaH', 'black elephant ???') }} If we read `kRSNagaja` as a `tatpuruSa`, then it just means "black elephant." But if we follow the pattern above, we get "something that *has* black elephants": !!! examples - {{ m.x('kRSNagaje nagare vasati', 'He lives in the city [that has] black elephants.') }} Traditionally, this type of compound is called a `bahu-vrIhi` ("much rice"). And the word `bahuvrIhi` itself is a `bahuvrIhi` compound: !!! examples - {{ m.x('[bahuvrIhiH] khAdati', '[The one who has much rice] eats.') }} Easy confusions --------------- It is very difficult to tell `bahuvrIhi` and `tatpuruSa` compounds apart. Here's an example from Kalidasa: !!! examples - {{ m.x('sUrya-prabhavaH', 'sun-origin') }} Is this "origin of the sun" or "one whose origin is the sun?" We can't tell. So let's look at the next word: !!! examples - {{ m.x('sUrya-prabhavo vaMzaH', 'sun-origin lineage') }} There are two ways we could read this; 1. "a lineage that is the origin of the sun" (`tatpuruSa`) 2. "a lineage whose origin is the sun" (`bahuvrIhi`)
