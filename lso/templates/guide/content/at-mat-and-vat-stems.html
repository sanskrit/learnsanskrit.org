`-mat` and `-vat` ----------------- `-mat` and `-vat` are stem suffixes. When added something that means X, they create words that mean "one that has X". If the original stem ends in `-a`, then `-vat` is used. Otherwise, `-mat` is used instead. Here are two examples you might recognize: !!! examples - {{ m.x('hanu + mat -> hanumat', 'jaw -> having (large) jaws; Hanuman') }} - {{ m.x('bhaga + vat -> bhagavat', 'glory -> having glory, glorious') }} Endings ------- ### Masculine The endings are the same as before. But note where the **strong** stems are used and where the weak stems are used: {% include 'guide/include/bhagavat-m.html' %} Here, the **strong** stems have black borders. We see that the strong stems are used in cases 1, 8, and 8. But when case 2 refers to many things, the weak stem is used instead. Note, too, that when case 1 refers to one thing, the stem is even stronger: it's `bhagavAn`, not `bhagavan`. ### Neuter As usual, everything here is weak: {% include 'guide/include/bhagavat-n.html' %} ### Feminine The feminine endings are `-matI` and `-vatI`. These follow the pattern of all other stems that end in `-I`. We'll see how these stems work in a later lesson.
