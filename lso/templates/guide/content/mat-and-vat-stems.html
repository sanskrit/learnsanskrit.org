"One that has X" ---------------- `-mat` and `-vat` are stem suffixes. When added something that means X, they create words that mean "one that has X". If the original stem ends in `-a`, then `-vat` is used. Otherwise, `-mat` is used instead. Here are two examples you might recognize: !!! examples - {{ m.x('hanu + mat -> hanumat', 'jaw -> having (large) jaws; Hanuman') }} - {{ m.x('bhaga + vat -> bhagavat', 'glory -> having glory, glorious') }} Endings ------- ### Masculine The endings are the same as before. The strong stems end in `-mant/-vant`, and the normal and weak stems both end in `-mat/-vat`: {% include 'guide/include/bhagavat-m.html' %} Here, the **strong** stems have black borders. We see that the strong stems are used in cases 1, 2, and 8. But when we use the plural of case 2, we use the weak stem instead. Note, too, that when case 1 refers to just one thing, the stem is even stronger: it's `bhagavAn`, not `bhagavan`. ### Neuter As usual, everything here is weak: {% include 'guide/include/bhagavat-n.html' %} Again, note that we add a nasal sound before the last consonant: we get `bhagavanti`, not *`bhagavati`. ### Feminine The feminine endings are `-matI` and `-vatI`. These follow the pattern of all other stems that end in `-I`. We'll see how these stems work in a later lesson.
