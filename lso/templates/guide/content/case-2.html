Review ------ Nouns can take different *roles* in some action. The simplest role just states *who performs the action*. This role is expressed by case 1: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gajaz carati', 'The elephant walks.') }} What does the action act upon? ------------------------------ Another useful role is **the "object" of the action**, and this is expressed by our next case: !!! examples - {{ m.x('[gajaM] pazyAmi', 'I see [the elephant].') }} - {{ m.x('[gajau] pazyasi', 'You see [the two elephants].') }} - {{ m.x('[gajAn] pazyAvaH', 'We all see [the elephants].') }} Since this is the second case we've seen, let's call this case 2 for short. Of course, we can define more than one role at the same time: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gajo [naraM] pazyati', 'The elephant sees [the man].') }} - {{ m.x('vIro [grAmAn] dizati', 'The hero points to [the villages].') }} And we can even change the order of these words: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gajo pazyati [naram]', 'The elephant sees [the man].') }} - {{ m.x('pazyati [naraM] gajaH', 'The elephant sees [the man].') }} - {{ m.x('[naraM] gajaH pazyati', 'The elephant sees [the man].') }} !!! note In the examples above, `gajaM` and `naraM` are originally `gajam` and `naram`. But they [blend]({{ m.lesson_url('anusvara-and-visarga') }}) with the sounds that follow them. ### Other meanings of case 2 When the action implies that something is moving, case 2 also defines the *destination* of the movement: !!! examples - {{ m.x('[naraM] gacchatha', 'You all go [to the man].') }} - {{ m.x('[narau] carAmi', 'I walk [to the two men].') }} - {{ m.x('[azvAn] gacchAvaH', 'The two of us go [to the horses].') }} ## Ambiguities in Sanskrit Did you notice that `gajau` is a word in both case 1 (`gajau pazyataH`) and case 2 (`gajau pazyAmi`)? This is a real ambiguity in Sanskrit; it's not immediately clear what case the noun uses, and in some sentences it might be impossible to tell: !!! examples -

{{ 'gajau pazyataH'|d }}

{{ 'gajau pazyataH'|i }}

(option 1) The two elephants see.
(option 2) The two of them see the two elephants.

{% filter markdown %} Blending`-n` ------------ At first, `-n` seems to blend in strange ways. But most of its odd changes are due to a few simple principles. As a reminder, don't try to memorize all of these rules. Read over them to get a sense of what's going on, then use the exercises at the bottom to practice. ### `n` unchanged `-n` is unchanged in front of vowels: !!! examples - {{ m.x('narAn azvo gacchati', 'The horse goes to the men.') }} - {{ m.x('gajAn icchasi', 'You want the elephants.') }} ### A hidden `s` In front of unvoiced stop consonants, `-n` is replaced by `-Ms`. This `s` then blends with the sounds that follow it. !!! examples - {{ m.x('grAmAn caranti -> grAmAMz caranti', 'They walk to the villages.') }} - {{ m.x('gajAn tudati -> gajAMs tudAmi', 'I strike the elephants.') }} ### Different points of sound In front of consonants that use the tongue, `n` changes its point of sound: !!! examples - {{ m.x('siMhAn jayasi -> siMhAJ jayasi', 'You conquer the lions.') }} - {{ m.x('vIrAn zarAH tudanti -> vIrAJ zarAs tudanti', 'The arrows strike the heroes.') }} ### Nasal vowels And in front of `l`, `n` does something slightly different. !!! examples - {{ m.x('azvAn labhe -> azvA~l labhe', 'I obtain the horses.') }} `~` indicates a nasal vowel; it is **not** pronounced as `m` or any other letter. In ordinary Sanskrit, this is the only place where `~` is used. ### Other changes This list of changes isn't complete, but it's good enough for now. {% endfilter %}
