"Characterized by X" -------------------- `-in`, `-min`, and `-vin` are stem suffixes. When added to something that means X, they create a stem that means something like "characterized by X." And if the original stem ends in `-a`, that `-a` is removed. Here's an example you might recognize: !!! examples - {{ m.x('yoga + in', 'yoga -> characterized by yoga; a yogi') }} `-in` can also be used as a root suffix. When added to a root that means Y, it creates a stem that means "one who does X". This causes the same consonant change that the usual `-a` suffix does: !!! examples - {{ m.x('tyaj + in -> tyAgin', 'abandon -> one who abandons') }} Endings ------- ### Masculine The endings are the same as before. The strong and normal stems end in `-in/-min/-vin`, and the weak stem ends in `-i/-mi/-vi`: {% include 'guide/include/yogin-m.html' %} Here, the **strong** stems have black borders. We see that the strong stems are used in cases 1, 8, and 8. But when case 2 refers to many things, the weak stem is used instead. Note, too, that when case 1 refers to one thing, the stem is even stronger: it's `yogI`, not `yogin`. I'm not sure why the `n` is removed. Finally, note where the weak stems are used only in front of consonants. ### Neuter As usual, everything here is weak: {% include 'guide/include/yogin-n.html' %} ### Feminine The feminine endings are `-inI`, `-minI`, and `-vinI`. These follow the pattern of all other stems that end in `-I`. We'll see how these stems work in a later lesson.
