Review ------ Sanskrit lets us make new words out of old ones. By learning to do this, we can quickly learn hundreds of new words and understand thousands more. In Sanskrit, we usually do this by sticking a *suffix* to the end of an old word: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gam -> gaccha -> gacchati', 'go (root) -> go (stem) -> She goes.') }} Stem suffixes ------------- A stem suffix is a suffix we attach to a noun or adjective stem instead of a verb root. There are dozens of different stem suffixes, but only a few are especially common. Here we'll study four of the most common stem suffixes. `-a` ---- `-a` creates all sorts of words. Generally, it creates adjectives that mean "coming from X." The first vowel is strengthened as much as possible: !!! examples - {{ m.x('putra -> pautraH', 'son -> "coming from a son," a grandson') }} - {{ m.x('kuru -> kauravaH', 'Kuru -> "coming from Kuru"; a Kaurava') }} - {{ m.x('pANDu -> pANDavaH', 'Pandu -> "coming from Pandu"; a Pandava') }} `-tva` ------ `-tva` creates neuter nouns that mean "X-ness". It causes no vowel changes: !!! examples - {{ m.x('kRSNa -> kRSNatvam', 'black -> blackness') }} - {{ m.x('sundara -> sundaratvam', 'beautiful -> beauty ("beautifulness")') }} - {{ m.x('nara + tva -> naratvam', 'man + `tva` -> "man-ness", manhood, humanity') }} `-maya` ------- `-maya` creates adjectives that mean "consisting of X" or "made of X". It causes no vowel changes: !!! examples - {{ m.x('Ananda -> AnandamayaH', 'bliss -> made of bliss') }} - {{ m.x('hiraNya -> hiraNyamayaH', 'gold -> made of gold, golden') }} `-ya` ----- `-ya` generally creates neuter abstract nouns. But the meanings can vary. The first vowel is strengthened as much as possible: !!! examples - {{ m.x('AcAra -> AcAryaH', 'rules, conduct -> one who knows the `AcAra`; an acharya') }} - {{ m.x('sundara -> saundaryam', 'beautiful -> beauty') }}
