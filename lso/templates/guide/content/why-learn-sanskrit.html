Before we begin, it's worth considering why we should learn Sanskrit at all. A history of motivations ------------------------ ### Religion and spirituality > From where did all this creation come?
> Whether He made it or whether He didn't,
> *He* surely knows, who sees all creation
> from the highest heaven.
> Or perhaps He doesn't know.
> Rigveda 10.129 (`nAsadIya sUkta`) Thousands of years ago, a language of great beauty and power arose on the Indian subcontintent. With this language as their thread, some of the greatest minds of that older India wove their meditations on every aspect of their world, their lives, and the nature of their existence. Together, these compositions are known as the [Vedas][vedas], the sacred lore of what we call Hinduism today. And of course, the language used in these compositions was Sanskrit. [vedas]: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedas As time went on, Sanskrit changed from a native language to a learned one. But because it was the language of this sacred lore, Sanskrit itself became a powerful and prestigious thing. So it was then the language of the great works that came after it, both as part of the Hindu tradition and as part of the other spiritual traditions of the time, like Buddhism and Jainism. Reading these texts in Sanskrit has an intimacy that doesn't easily appear when reading translations. But if you are not seriously invested in your tradition or its texts, then this motivation might not be strong enough. And depending on which tradition you follow, Sanskrit might even be unwise: > Seek [Govinda](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krishna), seek Govinda — > seek Govinda, you fool!
> For when you've come to your final hour, not even Sanskrit will save you.
> Bhaja Govindam ### Ancient knowledge > Now begins the instruction on words.
> Ashtadhyayi Because of its prestige and cultural power, Sanskrit soon became the common language of the Indian intellectual world — or at least, of a piece of it. Sanskrit was typically a language for upper-caste males, and Sanskrit works typically have little regard for the thoughts of women or the "lower" castes. Despite these shortcomings, the texts that these great minds have left behind are one of the surest views we have into the thoughts and views of the time. Reading the views of these older generations has a way of putting our own into useful perspective. (I'd like to highlight the Sanskrit linguistic tradition in particular. Its [crown jewel][ashtadhyayi] is a true work of genius, and its insights extend even [into the present day][generative].) [ashtadhyayi]: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pāṇini#Aṣṭādhyāyī [generative]: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generative_grammar But the discoveries of the modern world are fascinating too, and far more accessible. And the more common texts have translations available. Unless you're interested in the history of rarer kinds of Indian intellectual thought, this motivation might not be strong enough. ### Literature > All that is here is found elsewhere.
> But what's *not* here is nowhere else.
> [Mahabharata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahabharata) As time passed, the status of Sanskrit continued to grow. What was once just "language (`bhASA`) become "the perfected language" (`saMskRtA`) and, later, the "languge of the gods" (`deva-bhASA`). And as Sanskrit became more and more exalted, its literature and poetry became more and more intricate. All poetry is hard to translate, but Sanskrit poetry is *especially* difficult to translate. Its multiple shades of meaning, its elaborate double entendres, and its [ingenious feats of linguistic acrobatics][svadha] are dazzling to behold, and I know of no other language that allows all of them so fluidly. [svadha]: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shishupala_Vadha#Linguistic_ingenuity But I acknowledge that this can be an acquired taste. And since Sanskrit poetry can be so hard to translate, it can be hard to get a sense of what it's like unless you know a bit of the language already. ### Philology > The Sanscrit language, whatever be its antiquity, is of a wonderful structure; > more perfect than the Greek, more copious than the Latin, and more exquisitely > refined than either, yet bearing to both of them a stronger affinity … > than could possibly have been produced by accident; …
> Sir William Jones, in a speech delivered to the Asiatic Society in February 1786 When Western scholars first encountered Sanskrit, they found that Sanskrit was *unusually* similar to Greek and Latin. For a small taste of this, here are some common English words and their counterparts in Latin, Greek, and Sanskrit:

Notice that the words in all four languages are unusually similar. This is more than a coincidence: all of these languages share a common origin, which is also the origin of German, Persian, French, Bengali, and thousands of other languages across the globe. Together, these are called [Indo-European languages][iel], and Sanskrit is one of the oldest among them. Learning Sanskrit provides a deep and fascinating glimpse of the history behind all of these languages and how all language is destined to change over time. [iel]: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_languages] Resolve ------- Whatever your reason, it's important to have one, even if it's just that you're curious about how Sanskrit works. There are no limits to how little or how much time you can spend with the language. But whatever you choose to do, I hope that you find your Sanskrit journey to be a valuable one.
