Generally, every Sanskrit sentence is just some action. The simplest sentences are just actions and nothing else: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gacchati', 'He goes.') }} These sorts of actions are called verbs. `gacchati` is a verb. So are the words below: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gacchataH', 'The two of them go.') }} - {{ m.x('gacchanti', 'They go.') }} Stems and endings ----------------- `gacchati`, `gacchataH`, and `gacchanti` both start the same way, with `gaccha`. This part of the verb is called the stem. Just as many flowers bloom from the same plant stem, many verbs are formed from the same verb stem. We combine a stem with an ending to make a complete word: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gaccha + ti -> gacchati', 'He goes.') }} - {{ m.x('tiSTha + taH -> tiSThati', 'The two of them stand.') }} - {{ m.x('pazya + nti -> pazyanti', 'They see.') }} Number ------ A verb's ending describes the *number of things* that perform the action: !!! examples - {{ m.x('tiSThati', 'He stands.') }} - {{ m.x('pazyataH', 'The two of them see.') }} - {{ m.x('gacchanti', 'They go.') }} We can call this a change in the verb's number. Person ------ Not all verbs describes "he" and "they" and so on. We can talk about other sorts of people, too. We can talk about the people in front of us: !!! examples - {{ m.x('gacchasi', 'You go.') }} - {{ m.x('gacchathaH', 'The two of you go.') }} - {{ m.x('gacchatha', 'You all go.') }} And we can talk about ourselves: !!! examples - {{ m.x('tiSThAmi', 'I stand.') }} - {{ m.x('tiSThAvaH', 'The two of us stand.') }} - {{ m.x('tiSThAmaH', 'We all stand.') }} In English, this is called a change in the verb's person. This guide will use that term, too.
