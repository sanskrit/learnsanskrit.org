Review ------ We create *compounds* by removing a noun's ending and combining it with another noun. !!! examples - {{ m.x('[grAmavIraM] rakSAmi', 'I protect the [village hero].') }} We lose *freedom* and *clarity* by doing this, but we get a *density* of expression that is hard to achieve otherwise. The most common compound ------------------------ Now that we understand what compounds are and what they can do, let's study an example in more detail. This example uses the word `tasya`, which means "his": !!! examples - {{ m.x('tasya puruSaH → [tatpuruSaH]', 'His servant') }} The phrase on the right is the compound itself. The phrase on the left is called the {{ 'vigraha'|i('dfn') }} ("resolution"), and it rewrites the compound using normal case endings. The idea is that wherever the compound appears, the `vigraha` could be substituted and still communicate the **same meaning**. This is how compounds are traditionally discussed. Notice that the compound is produced of two separate parts. Almost all compounds are. Here, the two parts are `tat` ("that") and `puruSa` ("servant"). If a compound is a special type of its second part — if `vIragaja` is a special type of `gaja`, or if `devaloka` is a special type of `loka` — then the compound is called a {{ 'tatpuruSa'|i('dfn') }}. Thus the word `tatpuruSa` is itself a `tatpuruSa` compound, because `tatpuruSa` is a special type of `puruSa`. But not all compounds pass this test. Returning to English, a blockhead is *not* a special type of head; a blockhead is a person. And Blackbeard is not a special type of beard; Blackbeard is a pirate. ### Case 6 `tatpuruSa` The `tatpuruSa` is a rich compound that appears in many different forms. To help us talk about these forms, we can label a `tatpuruSa` compound based on the case that its first noun has in the `vigraha`. Consider the phrase below: !!! examples - {{ m.x('devasya putraH -> devaputraH', 'The son of the god') }} Here `deva` is in case 6 in the `vigraha`. So we would say that `devaputra` is a case 6 `tatpuruSa`. These sorts of compounds are in English, too: !!! examples - patch of cabbages → cabbage patch - leader of the world → world leader - bar of iron → iron bar Just as in Sanskrit, the first word loses its ending. For example, "cabbages" becomes just "cabbage." ### Case 1 `tatpuruSa` The case 1 `tatpuruSa` is a little more difficult to understand. An English example of it is "singer-songwriter," a person who is both a singer and a songwriter. Most often, it is used to pair a noun with an adjective: !!! examples - {{ m.x('kRSNo mRgaH → kRSNamRgaH', 'black deer') }} - {{ m.x('zvetAv azvau → zvetAzvau', 'two white horses') }} - {{ m.x('sundarAH siMhAH → sundarasiMhAH', 'beautiful lions') }} But it can pair nouns with nouns, too: !!! examples - {{ m.x('naro vIraH → naravIraH', 'man-hero; he who is both a man a hero') }} - {{ m.x('naraH siMhaH → narasiMhaH', 'man-lion; "he who is both a man and a lion," i.e. Narasimha') }} Notice that even without case endings, it is fairly easy to tell what the relationship between the two parts of each compound should be. As you read more Sanskrit, you will intuitively understand these compounds.
