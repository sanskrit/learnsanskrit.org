`a` and `A` ----------- The first sound in the Sanskrit alphabet is a simple one: {{ m.sounds( ('a', 'a', '"u" in "but"') )}} `a`, like "mutt" or "ruff" or "cummerbund." The sound comes naturally without any great effort. When you pronounce this sound, let your breath flow cleanly through your mouth, without any breaks or stops. Sounds produced in this way are called vowels. !!! note As you learn the Sanskrit alphabet, study the recordings carefully and consult the knowledgeable people around you. Use the English approximations only as a last resort. Now comes a second vowel: {{ m.sounds( ('A', 'aa', '"a" in "father"') )}} `A`, like "car" or "far" or "almost dark." `A` is **twice as long** as `a`. Vowels as long as `a` are called short, and vowels as long as `A` are called long. So when you pronounce `A`, make the sound loooong! Vowels in other places ---------------------- Sounds are formed by using different "places" in the mouth. "p" and "b" are puffs of air that explode from the *lips*, and "t" and "d" touch the tops of your *teeth*. Try pronouncing the English alphabet slowly, paying careful attention to how the sound *feels* and which parts of your mouth you use. `a` and `A` don't have a clear "place" in the mouth. But other vowels do. Push your tongue back and upward to produce these two sounds: {{ m.sounds( ('i', 'i', '"i" in "bit"'), ('I', 'ii', '"ee" in "teeth"') )}} `i` and `I`, like "fit" and "feet," or "lip" and "leap." `i` is *short* like `a`, and `I` is *long* like `A`. Let your tongue return to its original position. Then bring your lips together to produce these two sounds: {{ m.sounds( ('u', 'u', '"oo" in "mood" (short)'), ('U', 'uu', '"oo" in "mood" (long)'), )}} `u` and `U` both sound like the "oo" in "moon" and "spoon," but `u` is *short* like `a` and `i`. These first six vowels are familiar to us from English. But the next three vowels don't exist in English. Listen to the recordings carefully and pronounce them as well as you can: {{ m.sounds( ('R', 'r', '(no match)'), ('RR', 'rr', '(no match)'), ('lR', none, '(no match)') )}} `R` is *short* and `RR` is *long*. `R` is the "ri" in "Krishna" and "Sanskrit." If you don't know any Indian languages already, `R` and `RR` might be hard to pronounce. But after some practice, both sounds will become much easier. Meanwhile, `lR` is *short*. `lR` is also **extremely rare**. It is so rare, in fact, that it has no longer version, and people all throughout India pronounce it in various ways. I have not been able to find any consensus on how to pronounce it, so just use your best guess. Together, the nine vowels above (`a, A, i, I, u, U, R, RR, lR`) are called the simple vowels. Five are short (`a, i, u, R, lR`), and four are long (`A, I, U, RR`). Blending vowels --------------- In almost every text, *written Sanskrit is a perfect record of spoken Sanskrit*. To understand what this means, let's look at a simple Sanskrit sentence: !!! examples - {{ m.x('bAlAya Aha') }} He speaks for the boy. Try reading this sentence out loud ten times. As you might have noticed, it is tricky to keep stopping after `bAlAya` and keep starting again at `Aha`. Because of these pauses, speaking Sanskrit can feel slow. Fortunately, Sanskrit speakers avoided this problem by **blending sounds together**. Blended sounds are easier to pronounce and can be pronounced faster. In `bAlAya Aha`, for example, it is much easier to blend `a` and `A` and just say `bAlAyAha`. This is how Sanskrit is usually written down, too. Even in writing, vowels are blended wherever possible: !!! examples - {{ m.x('bAlAya Aha -> [bAlAyAha]', 'He speaks for the boy.') }} If you remember just one thing, remember that *written Sanskrit is a perfect record of spoken Sanskrit*. This idea is very important and will save us a lot of trouble. What's interesting is that the simple vowels often blend together quite nicely: !!! examples - {{ m.x('sA Apnoti -> sApnoti') }} She obtains. - {{ m.x('gacchati IzvaraH -> gacchatIzvaraH') }} The lord goes. - {{ m.x('madhu udakam -> madhUdakam') }} sweet water - {{ m.x('hotR RkAra -> hotRRkaraH') }} the `R` pronounced by the sacrificer How would you describe the changes that you see in the examples above? Take a moment to think of a rule that explains this behavior.
All of these changes are part of the same pattern. Let's say that vowels in the same "group" (like `i` and `I`) are similar. If two vowels are *similar*, then they merge to make a long vowel. This rule is clumsy to describe in English, but the intuition behind it should be much clearer.
