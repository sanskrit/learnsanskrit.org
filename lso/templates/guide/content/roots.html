This is the root: !!! examples - {{ m.x('khAd -> khAda -> khAdati', 'He eats.') }} Creating roots -------------- Just as a single tree trunk produces many branches and flowers, a single root produces many stems and words. But just as the base of the tree remains underground and hidden from view, so too is it difficult to tell what a root really is. In the examples above, the root is obviously `khAd`. But consider the examples below: !!! examples - {{ m.x('vIrA [jayanti]', 'The heroes [conquer].') }} - {{ m.x('putrau [jeSyataH]', 'The two sons [will conquer].') }} - {{ m.x('grAmo [jIyate]', 'The village [is conquered].') }} - {{ m.x('pazyAmi [jayakam]', 'I see [the victor].') }} - {{ m.x('loko [jitaH]', 'The world [has been conquered].') }} - {{ m.x('naro [jitvA] gacchati', 'The man goes [after conquering].') }} It is easy to think of a few possible roots. But consider the difficulties involved: 1. We must choose a single root that can produce `jay`, `je`, `jI`, and `ji`. 2. We should make rules to explain how our root transforms into `jay`, `je`, `jI`, and `ji`. 3. And these rules must be as consistent as possible across all roots and all of the stems that those roots produce. This is an enormous task. But fortunately, the grammarian Panini completed it for us more than 2000 years ago. Panini defined a full list of verb roots, together with the rules that transform those roots into the words we really see. He did not describe how he *chose* his roots, but by studying his list, we can create some simple guidelines. Here is one of them: !!! rule Roots should use simple and short vowels. We can then form stems by *strengthening* the vowel. Here, we strengthen a vowel by adding `a` to the front of it.
